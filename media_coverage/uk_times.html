Computers produce poetry by the metre

Mark Bridge, Technology Correspondent

July 31 2018, 12:01am, The Times

  • Books

Sonnets written by a computer are superior to Shakespeare’s in some respects — and the general public is unable to tell the difference between the two, researchers say.

Computer scientists have trained a “neural network” using 2,600 sonnets taken from a free online database and put the system to work writing its own poems, following the Shakespearean rhyming scheme.

In his Sonnet 18, Shakespeare asked: “Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day?/Thou art more lovely and more temperate.”

One of the artificial intelligence efforts begins: “He softly left from dark, of shining plain/and to the morn, to set her on the way.”

The team from IBM and the universities of Melbourne and Toronto asked non-specialists to distinguish between the computer-generated sonnets and the works of human poets and found they could not do so.

However, experts were not fooled. Adam Hammond, a co-author of the paper from the department of English at the University of Toronto, found the machine-generated poems to lack “readability” and “emotion”.

Nevertheless, he claimed that the machine was technically better at both rhyme and metre than human poets.

“This might seem surprising, but in fact it is well established that real poets regularly break rules of form to create other effects. In particular, there is evidence here that our focus on form actually hurts the readability of the resulting poems.”

Taken as a whole, the researchers said their work suggested that future efforts should look beyond forms and towards the substance of good poetry.

Artificial intelligence has previously been trained to write fairytales based on the works of the Brothers Grimm and even a chapter of an imagined Harry Potter book (Harry Potter and the Portrait of What Looked Like a Large Pile of Ash).

Some experts believe that computers will be able to write compelling poems, novels and film scripts within the next couple of decades. Others stress that today’s efforts are way off.

Michael Symmons Roberts, the poet and broadcaster, told New Scientist that the computer-generated sonnets were nowhere near recreating the “richness or complexity” of Shakespeare, or interweaving the layers of meaning and emotion that make a good poem. “At the moment, intriguing as it is, Shakespeare wins it by a country mile,” he said.
